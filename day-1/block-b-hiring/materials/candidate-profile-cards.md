# Candidate Profile Cards — Block B Role-Play Exercise

**Instructions:** Each student playing the candidate role receives ONE profile card. Play the character authentically, including their weaknesses. Do not show your card to the interviewer.

---

## Profile A — Priya (The Strong Candidate)

**Background:** 3 years at a mid-size SaaS company. Transitioned from software engineering to analytics. Comfortable with ambiguity and cross-functional work.

**Strengths:**
- Strong SQL and Python skills
- Clear communicator who explains technical work to non-technical audiences naturally
- Asks good clarifying questions before diving into problems

**Weaknesses:**
- Slightly overqualified for junior roles — may seem impatient with basic questions
- Can come across as too polished, which may mask areas where she's still growing

**The Tell:** Will proactively suggest improvements to the business question rather than just answering it. An interviewer with a good rubric should notice strategic thinking beyond what the role requires.

**How to Play This Profile:**
- Reframe vague questions into structured approaches
- Reference past stakeholder interactions naturally
- If asked a basic technical question, answer it correctly but quickly — show you want to move to harder problems
- When asked about collaboration, give a specific story about working with a PM to redefine a metric

## Profile B — Marcus (The Borderline Candidate)

**Background:** 2 years in a consulting firm doing analytics. Recently completed a data science bootcamp. Eager to move into a product analytics role.

**Strengths:**
- Solid technical execution — can write clean SQL, build dashboards, run analyses
- Strong work ethic and reliable delivery
- Good at following instructions and structured processes

**Weaknesses:**
- Struggles to explain findings to non-technical audiences
- Tends to jump to solutions without framing the problem first
- Asks few clarifying questions — assumes the question is straightforward

**The Tell:** When asked "How would you explain this to the VP of Marketing?" will default to technical jargon. A well-designed rubric with a communication dimension will catch this.

**How to Play This Profile:**
- Give technically correct but overly complex answers
- When asked to simplify, try but still use jargon ("so basically we'd run a logistic regression on the feature set")
- Answer questions directly without asking what the stakeholder actually needs to know
- Show enthusiasm and willingness to learn, but demonstrate the communication gap

## Profile C — Sarah (The Wrong-Level Candidate)

**Background:** 8 years in analytics. Led a team of 5 at a large enterprise. PhD in statistics. Applying because she wants a "fresh start" at a smaller company.

**Strengths:**
- Deep technical expertise and impressive resume
- Articulate about organizational challenges and team dynamics
- Can discuss advanced methodology confidently

**Weaknesses:**
- Too senior for a mid-level IC role — will be bored within 3 months
- Expects to influence strategy from day one
- May not be willing to do the hands-on execution the role requires

**The Tell:** Keeps steering conversation toward team building and strategy rather than hands-on execution. A good interviewer will notice the role mismatch.

**How to Play This Profile:**
- Answer questions about execution with "I would delegate that" or "I would design the framework for that"
- Mention your team management experience frequently
- When asked about a specific analysis, describe the approach at a high level but redirect to how you'd set up the process
- Express genuine interest in the company but subtly signal you see this as a stepping stone to management

## Profile D — Jordan (The Culture Fit Trap)

**Background:** 1.5 years at a trendy startup. Active on data Twitter/LinkedIn. Gives well-received conference talks. Known for great energy in interviews.

**Strengths:**
- Extremely likable and articulate
- Enthusiastic about the company and role
- Great cultural energy — would be fun to work with
- Uses the right vocabulary and frameworks

**Weaknesses:**
- Technical depth is shallow — analysis tends to be surface-level
- Has not worked with messy real-world data at scale
- Strong on "what" but weak on "how"

**The Tell:** When probed on methodology, pivots to storytelling about impact rather than explaining the approach. A rubric that separates "communication" from "technical depth" will catch this.

**How to Play This Profile:**
- Use buzzwords correctly but cannot go a level deeper when pressed
- When asked "how did you build that model?" respond with the business impact ("it drove a 15% improvement in retention") rather than the methodology
- If pressed on technical details, say something vague like "we used a pretty standard approach" and redirect
- Be warm, engaging, and make the interviewer like you — that's the trap
